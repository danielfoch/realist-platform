The workbook includes:

* **Estimated PSF Data**: annual estimates, 1985-2002
* **Charts**: price-band chart and precon-premium chart
* **Methodology**: assumptions and source URLs

The strongest hard anchors are Urbanation’s history: GTA new condo prices were **under $100/psf in 1981**, peaked at **$264/psf in 1989**, fell to **$156/psf in 1993**, and recovered toward **$300/psf by 2002**. ([Urbanation][1])

For the resale path, I used TRREB historical average MLS prices as the broad resale-market spine. TRREB’s annual average price went from **$109,094 in 1985** to **$273,698 in 1989**, then down to **$198,150 in 1996**, before recovering to **$243,255 in 2000**. ([GTA New Homes for Sale][2])

Important caveat: the **precon/new-condo PSF series has real anchors**, but the **resale condo PSF series is estimated**. I set 1989 resale condo PSF at **85% of new/precon PSF**, with a confidence band of **75%-95%**, then indexed that forward/backward using TRREB’s broad all-home resale average-price path. CMHC’s 1990s real MLS index supports the general shape of a prolonged resale correction, but it is not condo-only PSF data. ([Canada Mortgage and Housing Corporation][3])

The takeaway from this rough reconstruction: **precon likely moved from a normal new-product premium in 1989, to roughly parity or a small discount to resale around 1992-1994, then gradually rebuilt a premium as the new-condo market recovered.** A mid-90s sanity check is Camden Lofts, a small central Toronto project reportedly selling at roughly **$195/psf**, which lines up reasonably with the model’s mid-90s precon range.
