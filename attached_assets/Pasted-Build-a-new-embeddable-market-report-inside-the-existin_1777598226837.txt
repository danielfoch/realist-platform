2. Key Takeaways Section
Create four summary cards:

Card 1 — Inflation Risk Is Back
- CPI inflation rose to 2.4% in March 2026.
- Bank of Canada expects inflation to peak around 3% in April.
- Base case: inflation returns to 2% in early 2027 if oil prices decline.

Card 2 — Growth Is Modest
- Canadian GDP growth forecast:
  - 2026: 1.2%
  - 2027: 1.6%
  - 2028: 1.7%

Card 3 — Housing Remains Subdued

- Housing demand is limited by affordability challenges, slower population growth, and weak investor interest.
- Small-condo inventory overhang in some major centres is expected to restrain new construction.

Card 4 — Rate-Cut Story Is Complicated
- Weak economy supports lower-rate expectations.
- Higher oil-driven inflation risk makes the Bank of Canada more cautious.
- If oil remains high, inflation could stay near 3% for more than a year in the Bank’s illustrative scenario.

3. Main Narrative Section
Write the body as a market report with these sections:

Section A — “The Big Picture”
Explain:
- Before the war in the Middle East, Canada’s economy was broadly evolving as expected.
- Oil prices then surged.
- Gasoline prices pushed CPI inflation higher.
- The Bank’s outlook depends heavily on oil prices falling from roughly US$90 per barrel in Q2 2026 to US$75 by mid-2027.
- If oil does not fall, inflation could remain more persistent.

Section B — “Economic Growth: Slow, But Not Collapsing”
Include:
- GDP growth of 1.2% in 2026, 1.6% in 2027, and 1.7% in 2028.
- GDP growth is expected to gradually absorb excess supply.
- Output gap estimate: -1.5% to -0.5% in Q1 2026.
- Labour market indicators suggest slack.
- Unemployment has been around 6.5% to 7% over the past 12 months.
- Wage growth is generally between 3% and 3.5%.

Section C — “Inflation: Why the Bank Is Cautious”
Include:
- CPI inflation slowed to 1.8% in February, rose to 2.4% in March, and is expected to reach about 3% in April.
- Core inflation measures were near 2%: CPI-trim 2.2%, CPI-median 2.3%.
- Gasoline, transportation costs, food costs, fertilizer, shipping disruptions, and fuel surcharges are risks.
- Businesses’ short-term inflation expectations increased after the war began, while longer-term expectations remain anchored.

Section D — “Oil Prices: The Main Shock”
Include:
- Brent oil prices were trading around US$100 per barrel.
- Bank base-case assumption: Brent falls from US$90 in Q2 2026 to US$75 by mid-2027.
- Higher oil prices help Canada as a net energy exporter but hurt households through gasoline, food, and transportation costs.
- The benefit to Canada is muted compared with past oil booms because energy-sector investment is less responsive and more profits may flow to dividends.

Section E — “Interest Rates and Mortgages”
Include:
- Explain the tension:
  - Weak growth and housing softness point toward easier rates.
  - Higher inflation risk points toward caution.
- Do not predict a guaranteed rate cut.
- Explain that if inflation broadens beyond gasoline and food, the Bank may need to keep monetary policy tighter for longer.
- Include the Bank’s illustrative scenario: if oil prices stay around US$100 per barrel, inflation peaks around 3.1% in Q1 2027 and stays close to 3% for more than a year, requiring tighter monetary policy and consecutive policy-rate increases.

Section F — “Housing Market Implications”
Create subsections:

For Buyers:
- Do not rely only on future rate cuts.
- Stress-test affordability using current payments.
- Watch inflation and bond yields, not just Bank of Canada announcements.

For Sellers:
- Pricing discipline matters.
- Weak affordability means overpriced listings may sit.
- Rate relief may not arrive quickly enough to rescue poor pricing.

For Investors:
- Weak investor interest and condo inventory overhang are major warnings.
- The investment case must work on cash flow, financing costs, and holding power.
- Do not assume population growth will absorb all supply.

For Mortgage Renewers:
- Rate timing is the risk.
- Inflation cooling would help, but persistent oil prices could keep rates higher for longer.
- Renewal strategy should consider both fixed-rate certainty and variable-rate risk.

For Realtors:
- Explain the market as a tension between weak demand and inflation risk.
- Clients need to understand that housing softness does not automatically mean fast rate cuts.

Section G — “Trade, Tariffs, and CUSMA Risk”
Include:
- US tariffs and CUSMA uncertainty are weighing on exports, investment, and hiring.
- Average US tariff rate on Canadian goods in the outlook: 5.1%.
- Canadian tariff rate on US goods: 1.5%.
- The Bank says CUSMA outcomes are a major downside risk to growth and inflation.
- Trade restrictions could weaken business confidence, hiring, household spending, and housing demand.

Section H — “Bottom Line”
Use this final summary:
“The April 2026 Bank of Canada report does not point to a simple boom or crash narrative. It points to a market stuck between weak demand and renewed inflation risk. For Canadian real estate, that means affordability remains stretched, housing activity remains subdued, and rate relief may depend on whether oil prices and inflation pressures actually cool.”
