## Thumbnail Text

NO PERFECT TIME

## Script

RBC just asked Canadians when the perfect time is to buy a home.

And the answer was basically: it does not exist.

According to RBC's 2026 Home Ownership Poll, 64% of Canadians say you can never really know when the right time is to buy a home.

That sounds like a throwaway survey headline.

But I think it captures the exact psychology of this housing market.

People are not optimistic.

They are not confident.

They are not saying housing suddenly became affordable.

They are saying: maybe waiting for the perfect moment is pointless.

And that is a very different kind of buyer demand.

SCREENSHARE: Open the Realist report and show Chart 1, "Active buyers are more willing to act than the general population."

The first chart in the Realist report shows the split.

Among all Canadians, only 27% say now is the right time to buy.

But among people intending to purchase within the next two years, that jumps to 45%.

So active buyers are much more willing to act than the general population.

But this is where the interpretation matters.

That does not mean active buyers think everything is great.

It means the people actually trying to buy are looking at lower prices, somewhat better selection in some markets, and the possibility that this might be the least bad window they get.

That is not euphoria.

That is capitulation into action.

It is the buyer saying: I cannot time this thing perfectly, so I need to decide whether the deal works today.

And honestly, that is probably a better framework than waiting for some magical clean bottom that everyone can identify in real time.

Because if everyone can identify the bottom, it is probably not the bottom anymore.

SCREENSHARE: Move to Chart 2, "Where buyers think first-time buyers have an opening."

The second chart is the first-time buyer chart.

This is the part I think matters most.

Among active buyers, 46% agree that current market conditions are giving first-time buyers a chance to get into the market.

Ontario is above the national number at 37%.

That lines up with what we have been seeing in other data, including the Teranet report we covered recently.

First-time buyers are becoming a bigger share of the market in Ontario.

But that does not mean first-time buyers have it easy.

It means they are the ones most motivated to step into the knife-catching contest.

They have been the most marginalized group in the market for the last several years.

If you already own, you may be locked in.

You may have a cheap mortgage.

You may have equity.

You may not want to sell one expensive house just to buy another expensive house at a worse rate.

But if you are a first-time buyer, you do not have that same optionality.

Every year you wait, you are still renting.

Every year you wait, you are hoping prices fall faster than your life changes.

Every year you wait, you are asking whether the ownership train is getting further away from you.

So when the market softens, even a little, first-time buyers can become the eager buyers.

Not because the market is healthy.

Because they have felt shut out for so long that a slightly less terrible entry point starts to look like an opening.

SCREENSHARE: Move to Chart 3, "Canada does not agree on whether this is a buyer's market."

The regional split is also important.

National housing commentary keeps breaking because Canada does not have one housing market right now.

RBC asked people whether they think they are in a buyer's market or a seller's market.

Nationally, 27% say buyer's market and 36% say seller's market.

But in Ontario, 38% say buyer's market and only 24% say seller's market.

B.C. also has a stronger buyer's-market read.

Then you look at Quebec and Atlantic Canada, and the story flips.

In Quebec, 57% say seller's market.

In Atlantic Canada, 52% say seller's market.

That is why the national average is not enough.

Ontario and B.C. buyers are reacting to weaker resale conditions, softer pricing, and more negotiating room in some markets.

Other regions are still dealing with tighter conditions.

Same country.

Same affordability crisis.

Very different local market psychology.

So when someone says "Canadian housing is back" or "Canadian housing is dead," the first question should be: where?

Because the buyer sitting in Toronto is not seeing the same market as the buyer sitting in parts of Atlantic Canada.

SCREENSHARE: Move to Chart 4, "Buyers see opportunity, but confidence is weak."

Now here is the part that makes this less bullish.

Active buyers see a window, but their confidence is weak.

RBC found that 75% of active buyers say economic uncertainty is making them more cautious about buying a home.

72% say economic uncertainty is the biggest challenge to buying.

67% are worried it will affect their homebuying plans.

And fewer than half of active buyers feel confident making homebuying decisions in today's market.

So this is not a clean demand boom.

This is a stressed buyer pool.

They see opportunity, but they are not relaxed about it.

They are still worried about rates.

They are still worried about prices.

They are still worried about their job, their savings, and whether they are buying too early.

That is the market psychology right now.

Buyers are tired of waiting, but they are not exactly sleeping well.

SCREENSHARE: Move to Chart 5, "The cost of buying is forcing bigger trade-offs."

The trade-off chart is probably the ugliest one.

Among active buyers, 69% say they need to delay major purchases like a car or renovations.

62% are scaling back vacations.

60% say they need to completely overhaul their spending and saving habits.

57% say they need a side hustle or second job.

And 53% say retirement savings may be redirected toward a home purchase.

That last number is the quiet alarm bell.

When people start using future security to buy present shelter, that is not a healthy affordability story.

That is a system forcing households to stretch harder just to participate.

So yes, there may be more buyer activity.

Yes, some first-time buyers may be coming off the sidelines.

But the buyer base is still fragile.

If the buyer needs two incomes, family help, cutbacks, a side hustle, and maybe retirement savings just to buy, then sellers cannot just price off the fantasy of 2021.

The deal has to work against actual household math.

Not vibes.

Not a rate-cut dream.

Not a "housing always goes up" poster in a mortgage broker's office.

Actual payment math.

SCREENSHARE: Move to Chart 6, "Buyers still fear prices and rates can move against them."

The final chart shows why buyers feel so pressured.

RBC found that 56% of active buyers think home prices will rise this year.

49% think interest rates will rise this year.

So buyers are getting squeezed between two fears.

The first fear is buying too early and catching the falling knife.

The second fear is waiting too long and watching the window close.

That is a brutal place to make a decision from.

And it is why "can I time the market?" is the wrong question.

The better question is: can I survive being wrong?

If you buy and prices fall another 5%, can you still hold?

If rates do not fall as quickly as you hoped, does the payment still work?

If rent comes in lower than expected, does the investment still make sense?

If you lose a job, have a kid, or need to move, is there still a margin of safety?

That is the actual question.

Because nobody gets to perfectly time the housing market.

You get a local market, a mortgage payment, a down payment, a household income, and a downside case.

That is it.

So my read on the RBC poll is this:

Buyers are not suddenly confident.

They are exhausted.

They know the perfect time probably does not exist.

Some of them are starting to move anyway.

And first-time buyers may be the group most willing to step into the market because they have spent the last few years feeling like ownership was moving away from them.

That can create transactions.

It does not mean affordability is fixed.

It means the psychology has shifted from "wait for perfect" to "underwrite what is in front of you."

And honestly, that is probably the healthier mindset.

Do not buy because you think you nailed the bottom.

Buy because the numbers work, the downside is survivable, and the home or investment fits your actual life.

But survival matters more.
